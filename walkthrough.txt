  Section 3: The 19 Variants — The Experimental Dataset
                                                                                                                                  
  This section presents the raw data that everything else builds on. Kang et al. (2017, 2019) made 19 variants of PMDsc — the
  wild-type plus 18 mutants — and measured their kinetic parameters in vitro (kcat, Km, Ki) and their isopentenol production in   
  vivo (titer in mg/L).                                           

  What the kinetic parameters mean:

  - kcat (turnover number): how many substrate molecules the enzyme converts per second. Higher = faster enzyme.
  - Km (Michaelis constant): the substrate concentration at which the enzyme runs at half its maximum speed. Lower Km = tighter
  substrate binding. Think of it as the enzyme's "appetite" — low Km means it's efficient even at low substrate concentrations.
  - kcat/Km (catalytic efficiency): the overall measure of how good the enzyme is at catalysis. This iswhat enzyme engineers
  traditionally optimize.
  - Ki (substrate inhibition constant): the substrate concentration at which inhibition kicks in strongly. Higher Ki = the enzyme
  can tolerate more substrate before being inhibited. This turns out to be more important than kcat/Km for this system.
  - Titer: the actual amount of isopentenol produced in engineered E. coli. This is the bottom line — what we ultimately care
  about.

  Where the mutations are:

  The mutations span 11 positions on the enzyme. Some are in the active site itself (Y19, K22 — directly touch the substrate; S208
   — binds ATP), and some are nearby but not catalytic (R74 at the entrance, R147 and M212 on nearby helices, I145 in the interior
   of helix H2, I226 and V230 on helix H5). The location matters because mutations at catalytic residues tend to damage activity,
  while mutations at non-catalytic positions can modulate inhibition without killing the enzyme.

  The five key observations:

  1. Ki dominates titer, not kcat/Km. This is the single most important insight from the data. R74G is a terrible enzyme by
  conventional metrics (kcat/Km = 0.04, worse than wild-type's 0.066), yet it produces the second-most isopentenol (975 mg/L).
  Why? Because its Ki is 110 mM — the enzyme barely gets inhibited even at high MVAP concentrations. Conversely, V230E has
  reasonable catalytic efficiency but Ki = 10 mM, so the enzyme is heavily inhibited in vivo, producingonly 278 mg/L. The lesson:
   at the 100-200 mM MVAP concentrations inside the cell, what limits production isn't how fast the enzyme can turn over substrate
   — it's how much the substrate shuts the enzyme down.
  2. The GKQ paradox. R74G by itself relieves inhibition (Ki = 110). R147K and M212Q by themselves are catalytically beneficial
  mutations. But combining all three into GKQ (R74G-R147K-M212Q) produces Ki = 11 and a nearly dead enzyme in vivo (titer = 8).
  The mutations that should help individually are catastrophic together. This is a non-additive (epistatic) interaction, and
  explaining it is the central scientific question of the project.
  3. HKQ vs GKQ. These two triple mutants carry the same R147K and M212Q mutations. The only differenceis position 74: His in
  HKQ, Gly in GKQ. This single amino acid difference produces a 7-fold difference in Ki (80 vs 11) and a 135-fold difference in
  titer (1079 vs 8). Whatever is happening at position 74 is the key to the entire story.
  4. K22 mutations kill the enzyme. K22 directly contacts the substrate in the active site. Changing itto methionine or tyrosine
  drops titer to 22 mg/L regardless of everything else. This tells you that K22 is essential for catalysis — you can't touch it.
  5. Multi-mutant R74G combinations reduce L2 binding. Adding R147K to R74G (double mutant, titer 909) or R147K + Q140L (triple,
  titer 401) both show reduced secondary substrate binding. But importantly, R74G-R147K works well while adding M212Q on top
  (making GKQ) is catastrophic. The M212Q step is where things go wrong.

  Section 4: The Enzyme — Structural Context

  PMDsc belongs to the GHMP kinase superfamily (Galactokinase, Homoserine kinase, Mevalonate kinase, Phosphomevalonate kinase).
  The crystal structure (PDB code 1FI4) was solved at 2.27 Angstrom resolution in the apo (empty, no substrate bound) form. The
  protein has 396 residues.

  The active site is a pocket that accommodates three things simultaneously: the substrate (MVAP), ATP (the phosphate donor), and
  a magnesium ion (Mg2+, needed for catalysis). The catalytic mechanism involves D302 acting as a base (abstracting a proton),
  R158 facilitating decarboxylation (removal of CO2), and several serine residues (S121, S155, S208) coordinating ATP.

  The key conceptual division for this project is:                                                                                
  - Catalytic residues (K18, Y19, W20, K22, S120, S121, S153, S155, R158, S208, D302): these directly participate in binding
  substrate or performing chemistry. Mutating them risks killing the enzyme.                                                      
  - Non-catalytic positions near the active site (R74, R147, M212, I145, I226, V230): these shape the environment around the
  active site. Mutating them can change how the enzyme interacts with its surroundings — including how a second substrate molecule
   binds — without necessarily destroying catalytic activity.

  This distinction is critical for the project's logic: if you want to reduce substrate inhibition (change Ki) without killing the
   enzyme (maintain kcat/Km), you need to target the non-catalytic positions.

  Section 5: Computational Approach — How the Simulations Work

  This section describes the core methodology. Boltz-2 is a deep learning-based structure prediction tool (similar in concept to
  AlphaFold) that can predict protein-ligand complex structures. The key innovation here is using it tocofold the enzyme with two
   MVAP molecules simultaneously, to find where the second (inhibitory) substrate binds.

  The setup:

  - L1 (ligand 1): constrained to the active site using "pocket conditioning" — essentially telling Boltz-2 "place this MVAP in
  the known catalytic pocket." L1 represents the substrate that's being processed.
  - L2 (ligand 2): completely unconstrained — Boltz-2 is free to place it anywhere on the protein surface. L2 represents the
  inhibitory second substrate molecule. Where Boltz-2 places L2 is the prediction of where the secondary binding site is.

  For each of the 19 variants, 50 independent Boltz-2 models were generated (950 total). For each model, the analysis measures:

  1. L2-L1 distance: how far apart the two MVAP molecules are (center-of-mass to center-of-mass). If L2ends up far from L1, it
  probably just landed at a random spot on the protein surface. If L2 is close to L1 (< 15 Angstroms), it found the secondary
  binding site.
  2. L2 contact residues: which protein residues are within 4 Angstroms of L2. This tells you what the secondary binding site
  looks like.
  3. Binding mode: is L2 sitting at the entrance to the active site (non-competitive inhibition) or inside the active site itself
  competing with L1 (competitive inhibition)?
  4. iPTM confidence: Boltz-2's own confidence score for the L2 placement. Higher iPTM = Boltz-2 is more confident about where it
  put L2.

  Additionally, single-MVAP structures (50 per variant, no L2) were generated as references. These are used to study how different
   mutations change the enzyme's structure in the absence of the inhibitor — for example, how H74 pushes loop 17-33 closed.

  Section 6: The Secondary Binding Site — Where the Inhibitor Binds

  This is the first major finding. Across all 19 variants, when Boltz-2 places L2 close to the active site, it consistently lands
  in the same place: the mouth of the active-site channel. This is a shallow depression where the substrate normally pauses before
   entering the active site.

  Two distinct inhibition mechanisms:

  1. Entrance binding (non-competitive inhibition): L2 parks at the entrance, blocking the door. It contacts gateway residues
  (position 74, K22, T209) but doesn't go inside. This is like someone standing in a doorway — they don't take your seat, but they
   prevent you from getting through. The catalytic machinery inside is fine, but new substrate can't get in. Because the inhibitor
   doesn't need to compete for the same binding site as the substrate, this type of inhibition is effective at relatively low
  inhibitor concentrations — hence low Ki.
  2. Active-site binding (competitive inhibition): L2 actually enters the active site and competes withL1 for the catalytic
  pocket. This is like someone taking your seat — but they have to beat you to it. Because the inhibitor has to outcompete the
  substrate (which is already bound and being processed), it takes much higher concentrations to achieve inhibition — hence high
  Ki.

  This distinction is crucial. If you just count "how many models have L2 close to L1" (total close-binding), you lump both
  mechanisms together. But they have opposite implications for Ki: entrance binding = low Ki (bad), active-site binding = high Ki
  (less bad). That's why the guide uses entrance-binding fraction as the primary metric.

  The L2 placement table ranks all 19 variants by entrance-binding fraction. The key patterns:

  - Variants at the top (I145F 48%, S208E 42%, V230E 40%) have high entrance binding — the inhibitor frequently lands at the
  entrance. V230E, the one with measured Ki = 10 (the strongest inhibition), is right at the top.
  - Variants at the bottom (HKQ 2%, GKQ 4%, R74G-R147K-Q140L 4%) have minimal entrance binding. HKQ (Ki= 80) effectively blocks
  the entrance.
  - R74G (Ki = 110) has only 6% entrance binding but 12% active-site binding. Its inhibition is competitive, which explains its
  high Ki despite having some close-binding overall.

  Consensus contact residues — looking across all 271 close-binding models from all 19 variants, the same residues show up over
  and over:

  - R/G/H74 (80% of models), K22 (79%), T209 (79%): these three define the entrance site. They're contacted in ~4 out of every 5
  close-binding models.
  - N28 (61%), N72 (56%): secondary anchors flanking the entrance.
  - S153 (50%), S208 (47%): catalytic residues that border the entrance — important for understanding the site geometry but can't
  be mutated without killing catalysis.

  The secondary site is not a deep, well-defined pocket like a traditional allosteric site. It's a shallow surface depression at
  the channel mouth. Under normal conditions, substrate passes through quickly. At saturating concentrations, a second molecule
  gets stuck here and blocks traffic.

  Section 7: Single-Mutation Effects — Dissecting Individual Contributions

  This section examines what each mutation does individually before looking at combinations. The 14 single mutants (plus the 5
  original variants) let you isolate the effect of each position.

  Position 74 (the entrance gateway): The most important position. Three amino acids are tested:
  - Arg (wild-type): 28% entrance binding. The long, positively charged sidechain acts as an electrostatic trap for MVAP's
  negatively charged phosphate. Ki = 18.
  - Gly (R74G): 6% entrance, 12% active-site. No sidechain at all — the trap is gone, but so is any barrier. The inhibition mode
  switches to competitive. Ki = 110.
  - His (R74H): 10% entrance, 4% active-site. The bulky imidazole ring physically pushes the loop closed. Ki not measured but
  titer (770) is much better than WT (475).

  Position 22 (the loop anchor): K22M and K22Y both collapse titer to 22 mg/L. K22 is essential for catalysis. The
  entrance-binding fractions (18% and 24%) aren't dramatically different from WT, so the problem isn't inhibition — it's that the
  enzyme can't work without K22.

  Position 145 (helix H2 interior): I145F increases entrance binding to 48% — the highest of any variant. The bulky phenylalanine
  apparently reshapes the entrance channel to make it more attractive to L2. This tells you that I145 is important for maintaining
   the entrance geometry, and you should not mutate it in the HKQ background (it would undo H74's entrance closure).

  Position 147 (helix H2 surface): R147K has the same entrance-binding fraction as WT (28%) but much better titer (793 vs 475).
  Its benefit is purely catalytic — it improves kcat/Km from 0.066 to 0.32 without changing the inhibition landscape.

  Position 212 (helix H4): M212Q alone actually increases entrance binding (36% vs WT's 28%). As a single mutation, it does not
  help with inhibition. Its inhibition-relieving effect only manifests in the HKQ combination, where itforms the Q212 bridge
  (Section 10). In the GKQ combination, it actually makes things worse.

  Entrance-adjacent positions: T209D slightly reduces entrance binding (26% vs 28%). S208E dramaticallyincreases both entrance
  (42%) and active-site (28%) binding — disrupting this catalytic residue reshapes the entire pocket. S186C increases entrance
  binding to 36%.

  Distal positions: V230E has 40% entrance binding consistent with its very low Ki (10). I226V shows 24% entrance and 12%
  active-site binding.

  The section's overall lesson: individual mutations have modest, predictable effects. The dramatic phenomena (GKQ collapse, HKQ
  success) only emerge from combinations — they are epistatic (non-additive) effects.

  Section 8: The R74 Gateway Mechanism — Three Amino Acids, Three Strategies

  This section explains the physical mechanism by which position 74 controls substrate inhibition. It'sthe mechanistic heart of
  the story.

  Arginine (WT) — the electrostatic trap:                                                                                         
                                                                                                                                
  Arginine has a long, flexible sidechain terminating in a positively charged guanidinium group. MVAP carries a negatively charged
   phosphate group. Opposite charges attract. When a second MVAP approaches the entrance, R74's positive charge reaches out and
  grabs the phosphate via a hydrogen bond (2.3 Angstroms). The entrance becomes a sticky trap — the second substrate molecule gets
   caught there and blocks the channel. This is effective inhibition: Ki = 18 mM.

  Crucially, R74 makes zero physical contacts with loop 17-33 (the loop that forms one wall of the entrance). It doesn't close the
   entrance — it just makes the entrance attractive to the substrate.

  Glycine (R74G) — the open gate:

  Glycine is the smallest amino acid — it has no sidechain at all (just a hydrogen atom). With R74G, the electrostatic trap is
  gone. There's nothing at position 74 to grab the substrate. But there's also nothing to block entry. The entrance is wide open.
  L2 can occasionally drift all the way into the active site and compete with L1, but this is inefficient because the substrate
  has to outcompete an already-bound molecule. The result is competitive inhibition with Ki = 110 mM. High Ki, but through a
  fundamentally different mechanism — not because the enzyme resists inhibition, but because the remaining inhibition mode
  (competitive) is inherently weaker.

  Histidine (R74H and HKQ) — the closed door:

  Histidine has a compact, bulky imidazole ring. Unlike arginine, which reaches out toward the substrate, histidine pushes inward
  toward the loop. It makes 21 direct van der Waals contacts with loop residues T25, K26, and N28. These contacts physically push
  loop 17-33 closed over the entrance, like shutting a door. The substrate can't get in.

  This is the most effective strategy: R74H alone reduces close-binding from 32% to 14%. In HKQ (with the added Q212 bridge), only
   1 out of 50 models places L2 near the active site.

  The key insight: Arg and His both reduce inhibition compared to having no gatekeeper, but through opposite physics:
  - Arg attracts the inhibitor (trap) — the inhibitor binds, but the trapping still causes inhibition.
  - His blocks the inhibitor (door) — the inhibitor can't bind at all, so no inhibition.

  For production purposes, blocking is better than trapping. A closed door prevents inhibition entirely. A trap still holds the
  inhibitor in place where it can block substrate turnover.

  Section 9: R74G and Competitive Inhibition — A Different Mode

  This section examines R74G's close-binding models in detail. When L2 does get close in R74G models, it's qualitatively different
   from other variants: L2 doesn't sit at the entrance — it goes all the way inside the active site, contacting 10 catalytic
  residues (K18, Y19, W20, K22, S121, S153, S155, R158, S208, D302) and physically displacing L1.

  This is textbook competitive inhibition: two molecules competing for the same site. Competitive inhibition requires high
  inhibitor concentrations because the inhibitor must outcompete the substrate that's already there. This explains R74G's high Ki
  (110 mM) despite having some close-binding.

  All G74-containing variants (R74G, R74G-R147K, GKQ, R74G-R147K-Q140L) show this active-site binding mode. By contrast, WT (with
  R74) almost never shows it — only 2 out of 16 close-binding models have active-site binding (4%). Theexception is S208E, which
  shows 28% active-site binding despite retaining R74 — because mutating the catalytic S208 to Glu disrupts the active-site
  geometry so much that it creates an opening for L2 to enter.

  Section 10: The GKQ Paradox — Why Adding Good Mutations Makes Things Worse

  This is the central puzzle and arguably the most intellectually interesting part of the project.

  The paradox laid out:

  ┌─────────────────────────┬────────────────────────┬──────┬───────┬──────────────────────────────┐
  │          Step           │        Variant         │  Ki  │ Titer │        What happened         │
  ├─────────────────────────┼────────────────────────┼──────┼───────┼──────────────────────────────┤
  │ Start                   │ R74G                   │ 110  │ 975   │ Great Ki, poor catalysis     │
  ├─────────────────────────┼────────────────────────┼──────┼───────┼──────────────────────────────┤
  │ Add R147K               │ R74G-R147K             │ N.D. │ 909   │ Better catalysis, still good │
  ├─────────────────────────┼────────────────────────┼──────┼───────┼──────────────────────────────┤
  │ Add M212Q               │ GKQ (R74G-R147K-M212Q) │ 11   │ 8     │ Catastrophe                  │
  ├─────────────────────────┼────────────────────────┼──────┼───────┼──────────────────────────────┤
  │ But with H instead of G │ HKQ (R74H-R147K-M212Q) │ 80   │ 1079  │ Best variant                 │
  └─────────────────────────┴────────────────────────┴──────┴───────┴──────────────────────────────┘

  R147K and M212Q are individually beneficial mutations. Adding them to R74G should help. And they do help with catalysis (kcat/Km
   goes from 0.04 to 0.50). But they catastrophically worsen inhibition (Ki drops from ~high to 11). The same two mutations
  combined with R74H instead of R74G produce the best variant ever.

  The Q212 bridge explains why:

  The M212Q mutation replaces methionine (a nonpolar amino acid with a sulfur-containing sidechain) with glutamine (a polar amino
  acid with an amide group that can donate and accept hydrogen bonds). This amide group is the key player.

  In HKQ (with H74): H74's 21 contacts push loop 17-33 into a specific conformation. In this conformation, Q212's amide group is
  perfectly positioned to form hydrogen bonds with two residues on the loop: Y19 (2.72 A) and K22 (2.79A). This creates a bridge
  connecting helix H4 (where Q212 sits) to loop 17-33 (where Y19 and K22 sit). The bridge locks the loop in its closed position —
  it can't swing open because it's tethered. Combined with H74's physical contacts, the entrance is doubly secured: closed door +
  locked loop.

  In GKQ (with G74): G74 has no sidechain, so the loop adopts a different conformation. In this conformation, Q212 is too far from
   Y19 and K22 to form the bridge (distances > 6 Angstroms, well beyond hydrogen-bond range). The bridge does not form despite the
   identical M212Q mutation being present. Instead, Q212's amide group is free — it's not tied up in the bridge. When L2
  occasionally visits the entrance, this free amide group can form hydrogen bonds directly with L2, stabilizing the inhibitor at
  the entrance. In other words, the same mutation that protects the entrance in HKQ actively helps the inhibitor bind in GKQ.

  The K147 salt bridge also behaves differently: in HKQ, K147 forms a salt bridge with E144 (2.5 A), stabilizing helix H2. In GKQ,
   K147 points away from E144 toward H176 instead. Same mutation, different structural context, different behavior.

  Why GKQ fails — the full picture:

  1. G74 leaves the entrance physically open (no barrier).
  2. The Q212 bridge doesn't form (loop is not locked).
  3. R147K and M212Q restore the catalytic geometry that R74G alone had disrupted — but this also restores the entrance site's
  shape.
  4. Q212's free amide stabilizes L2 when it visits the entrance.

  The result: a fast enzyme (good kcat/Km) that is easily inhibited (low Ki) — the worst possible combination for in vivo
  production.

  The frequency vs affinity distinction:

  There's an apparent contradiction: GKQ's entrance-binding fraction from Boltz-2 is only 4% — lower than WT's 28%. If entrance
  accessibility determined Ki, GKQ should have high Ki. But Ki = 11 (low). The resolution is that Boltz-2 measures geometric
  accessibility (how often L2 lands at the entrance) but not thermodynamic affinity (how tightly L2 binds once it's there). GKQ's
  entrance site is rarely visited (4%) but when L2 does land there, Q212 stabilizes the interaction, making it thermodynamically
  favorable. The entrance is a rare but effective trap.

  The R74G-R147K intermediate supports this interpretation: it's the same as GKQ but without M212Q, so position 212 is methionine
  (no H-bond donor). Without Q212's stabilizing amide, L2 can't be trapped at the entrance even when itvisits. Titer = 909,
  nearly as good as R74G alone.

  Section 11: Why HKQ Wins — The Best of Both Worlds

  HKQ achieves the highest titer (1079 mg/L) because it simultaneously solves both challenges:

  Challenge 1 — catalytic efficiency: R147K and M212Q together improve kcat/Km from 0.066 (WT) to 0.40 — a 6-fold improvement. The
   enzyme is much better at processing MVAP.

  Challenge 2 — substrate inhibition resistance: Three layers of protection work together:

  1. H74 closes the entrance (21 contacts to loop 17-33 physically seal the channel mouth) — the door.
  2. Q212 bridge locks loop 17-33 (H-bonds to Y19 and K22 prevent the loop from reopening) — the deadbolt.
  3. K147-E144 salt bridge (stabilizes helix H2, adding rigidity to the entrance region) — the doorframe.

  Only 1 out of 50 Boltz-2 models places L2 anywhere near the active site. Ki = 80 mM.

  Why is HKQ's Ki (80) lower than R74G's (110)? Because they use different strategies. R74G eliminates the entrance site entirely
  — there's nothing there for the inhibitor to bind. The only inhibition mode left is competitive (active-site), which is
  inherently weak. HKQ merely closes the entrance — the site still exists structurally, just blocked. Under extreme conditions,
  the door might occasionally crack open. But for in vivo production, HKQ's combination of good catalysis (kcat/Km = 0.40) and
  strong Ki (80) is far superior to R74G's high Ki (110) with terrible catalysis (kcat/Km = 0.04).

  The ranking table shows all 19 variants ordered by entrance-binding fraction alongside their Ki, kcat/Km, and titer. The best
  production variants cluster at low entrance binding + adequate catalytic efficiency: HKQ (2% entrance, 0.40 kcat/Km), R74G-R147K
   (6% entrance, 0.42), R74H (10% entrance, 0.44).

  the three protective mechanisms (entrance anchor type, loop closure contacts, Q212 bridge).

  Section 12: Correlations and Statistical Analysis

  This section puts numbers on the relationships described qualitatively above.

  Ki prediction accuracy: The entrance-binding model correctly predicts Ki rank order for 4 of 5 variants with measured Ki:
  - V230E (40% entrance) → lowest Ki (10). Correct.
  - WT (28% entrance) → moderate Ki (18). Correct.
  - HKQ (2% entrance) → high Ki (80). Correct.
  - R74G (6% entrance but mostly competitive mode) → highest Ki (110). Correct — the mechanism classification resolves this.
  - GKQ (4% entrance) → should predict high Ki, but actual Ki = 11. Wrong. This is the one failure, explained by the
  frequency-vs-affinity distinction (Q212 stabilization).

  Correlation statistics: Entrance-binding fraction gives Pearson r = -0.60 with Ki (better than total close-binding at r = -0.45
  or mean distance at r = 0.33). The p-values are not significant (0.28), but with only n = 5 data points, significance is
  extremely hard to achieve. The important point is that removing GKQ gives a perfectly monotonic rank ordering for the remaining
  4 variants.

  Group comparison: High-titer variants (>= 700 mg/L) have roughly half the entrance-binding fraction (10.4%) of low-titer
  variants (< 400 mg/L, 20.4%). The Mann-Whitney p = 0.295 is not significant, but the trend is in the expected direction.

  Titer regression model: A model using kcat/Km + entrance fraction gives R² = 0.045 — essentially no predictive power. This
  confirms that titer is affected by many factors beyond enzyme kinetics (protein expression level, folding stability, cellular
  metabolic context). You can't predict titer from kinetics alone, but you can predict which mutations will relieve substrate
  inhibition.

  Section 13: Proposed Mutations — What to Try Next

  Based on the consensus contact residues from 271 close-binding models, three mutations are proposed to further disrupt the
  secondary binding site:

  1. T209A or T209V (priority 1): T209 contacts L2 in 79% of all close-binding models — it's the strongest non-catalytic L2
  contact. It forms a hydrogen bond to MVAP's phosphate. Replacing it with a non-polar residue (Ala or Val) should eliminate this
  H-bond. The existing T209D mutant retains 28% close-binding, suggesting that the negative charge of aspartate partially
  compensates for the lost hydroxyl — so a non-polar replacement is preferred. Caution: T209 is adjacent to catalytic S208, so you
   need to verify that S208 positioning isn't perturbed.
  2. N72A or N72V (priority 2): N72 contacts L2 in 56% of close-binding models. It H-bonds MVAP's carboxylate from the opposite
  side of the entrance. Removing this H-bond should weaken L2 binding at the entrance.
  3. N28A or N28V (priority 3): N28 contacts L2 in 61% of models. It's a loop 17-33 anchor for L2. Higher frequency than N72 but
  ranked lower because mutating a loop residue carries more risk of perturbing loop conformation.

  The ideal next-generation variant would be HKQ + one or more of these mutations: keep HKQ's triple defense (H74 closure, Q212
  bridge, K147 salt bridge) and add entrance-disrupting mutations on top.

  The section also notes that position 145 mutations should be avoided in the HKQ background (I145F increases binding to 58%),
  that V230E serves as a useful negative control (high binding, low Ki), and that S208E is informative but untargetable because
  S208 is catalytic.

  ---
  Section 14: Limitations — What We Can and Can't Conclude

  The guide is honest about six limitations:

  1. Statistical power: 50 models per variant is enough for entrance-binding fractions, but only 5 variants have measured Ki
  values — too few for significant correlations. The model works for 4 of 5 variants but can't be rigorously validated.
  2. Geometry vs thermodynamics: Boltz-2 tells you where L2 can land but not how tightly it binds. GKQ shows this matters — 4%
  entrance binding but Ki = 11 because of Q212 stabilization. Full free energy calculations (FEP/TI) would be needed to predict Ki
   quantitatively.
  3. No dynamics: Boltz-2 produces frozen snapshots. In reality, the entrance opens and closes dynamically. H74's 21 contacts in a
   static structure might be fewer in solution when the loop fluctuates.
  4. No binding energies: The analysis identifies the site and its contacts but cannot calculate binding affinity.
  5. Missing Ki data: Measuring Ki for R74H, R147K, M212Q, I145F, and I145A would provide much strongerstatistical power for
  testing the entrance-binding hypothesis.
  6. Titer confounders: Titer depends on protein expression, folding, and cellular metabolism — not just enzyme kinetics. The weak
   titer-entrance correlation (r = -0.21) partly reflects these additional factors.

  The overall narrative arc of the guide:

  The problem is substrate inhibition (Sections 1-2). A second MVAP molecule binds the enzyme and shutsit down. The experimental
  data (Section 3) shows that Ki dominates titer and reveals the GKQ paradox. The computational approach (Section 5) uses Boltz-2
  cofolding to find where the inhibitor binds. The secondary binding site is at the entrance to the active-site channel (Section
  6), and there are two distinct inhibition mechanisms — entrance binding (non-competitive, low Ki) andactive-site binding
  (competitive, high Ki). Position 74 is the master switch (Sections 7-9): Arg traps the inhibitor, Glyopens the gate, His closes
   the door. The GKQ paradox (Section 10) is explained by the Q212 bridge — the same M212Q mutation either protects the entrance
  (with H74) or stabilizes the inhibitor (with G74), depending on the conformational context set by position 74. HKQ wins (Section
   11) because it combines catalytic efficiency with a triple-defense entrance closure. The analysis correctly predicts Ki for 4
  of 5 variants (Section 12), and the one failure (GKQ) reveals the distinction between geometric accessibility and thermodynamic
  affinity. Proposed mutations (Section 13) target the top consensus contacts at the entrance to further weaken inhibitor binding
  in the HKQ background.
